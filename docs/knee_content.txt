SPECIAL CONFIRMATORY TESTS
Tests for PATELLOFEMORAL PAIN SYNDROME
1. Resisted Quadriceps Test
a. Patient is seated with knee slightly flexed. Examiner applies resistance as the patient extends the knee.
b. Sign is positive if pain is felt over the anterior patella.
If positive, indicates patellofemoral joint irritation associated with PFPS.
2. Step-Down Test
a. Patient stands on a 20 cm step and slowly lowers the contralateral heel to the ground while examiner observes knee alignment.
b. Sign is positive if there is pain over the anterior knee or if the knee collapses medially (dynamic valgus).
If positive, suggests PFPS and poor lower limb control.
3. Clarke’s Test (Patellar Grind Test)
a. Patient is supine with knee extended. Examiner applies gentle downward pressure on the superior pole (base) of the patella while patient contracts the quadriceps.
b. Sign is positive if anterior knee pain is reproduced.
If positive, indicates patellofemoral joint dysfunction and PFPS.
4. Patellar Apprehension Test
a. Patient is supine and relaxed. Examiner pushes the patella laterally.
b. Sign is positive if patient shows apprehension or contracts the quadriceps.
If positive, suggests lateral patellar instability, which may co-exist with PFPS.
5. Patellar Tilt Test
a. Patient is supine with knee extended. Examiner lifts the lateral edge of the patella.
b. Sign is positive if lateral patellar edge cannot reach horizontal.
If positive, suggests tight lateral structures contributing to PFPS.
6. Waldron Test
a. Patient is seated and actively flexes and extends the knee while examiner palpates the patellofemoral joint.
b. Sign is positive if grinding, crepitus, or anterior knee pain is felt.
If positive, indicates PFPS or patellofemoral joint irritation.
7. Patellar Glide Test
a. Patient is supine with knee extended. Examiner glides the patella medially and laterally.
b. Sign is positive if there is pain or abnormal patellar mobility.
If positive, suggests patellar maltracking and PFPS.

SPECIAL CONFIRMATORY TESTS
Tests for Meniscal Tears
1. McMurray Test
a. Patient is supine. Examiner flexes the hip and knee fully, then rotates the tibia internally and externally while extending the knee.
b. Sign is positive if there is a palpable click, snap, or pain along the joint line.
If positive, suggests a medial or lateral meniscus tear, depending on rotation (internal = lateral meniscus, external = medial meniscus).
2. Apley’s Compression Test
a. Patient lies prone with knee flexed to 90°. Examiner applies downward pressure along the tibia while rotating it internally and externally.
b. Sign is positive if pain is elicited along the joint line during compression.
If positive, indicates meniscal injury.
3. Thessaly Test
a. Patient stands on one leg with knee slightly flexed (~20°). Patient rotates the body and knee internally and externally three times.
b. Sign is positive if pain or a sense of locking/catching occurs along the joint line.
If positive, suggests a meniscal tear.
4. Joint Line Tenderness
a. Examiner palpates along the medial and lateral joint lines of the knee while patient is relaxed.
b. Sign is positive if localized tenderness is felt.
If positive, indicates possible meniscal pathology.
5. Bounce Home Test
a. Patient is supine. Examiner fully flexes the knee and then allows it to passively extend.
b. Sign is positive if the knee does not fully extend (blocked) or produces a springy end-feel.
If positive, suggests a displaced meniscal fragment (e.g., bucket-handle tear).
6. Ege’s Test (Weight-Bearing Variant)
a. Patient stands with knees flexed and externally rotates both feet to test the medial meniscus; internally rotates both feet to test the lateral meniscus.
b. Sign is positive if pain or clicking occurs along the joint line.
If positive, suggests meniscal tear.

SPECIAL CONFIRMATORY TESTS – KNEE LIGAMENTS
Tests for MCL Injury
1. Valgus Stress Test
a. With pt supine, knee flexed to 30°, examiner stabilizes the femur and applies a valgus force to the knee.
b. Positive sign = Pain or gapping at the medial knee joint line indicates MCL injury.
2.Grade Assessment via Valgus Stress
a. Grade I: Pain without laxity (<5 mm)
b. Grade II: Pain + moderate laxity (5–10 mm)
c. Grade III: Pain + marked laxity (>10 mm)
Tests for LCL Injury
1.Varus Stress Test
a. With pt supine, knee flexed to 30°, examiner stabilizes the femur and applies a varus force to the knee.
b. Positive sign = Pain or gapping at the lateral knee joint line indicates LCL injury.
2. Grade Assessment via Varus Stress
a. Grade I: Pain without laxity (<5 mm)
b. Grade II: Pain + moderate laxity (5–10 mm)
c. Grade III: Pain + marked laxity (>10 mm)
Tests for ACL Injury
1. Lachman Test
a. Knee flexed ~30°, examiner stabilizes femur and pulls tibia anteriorly at the knee.
b. Positive sign = Soft endpoint or increased anterior tibial translation indicates ACL tear.
2. Anterior Drawer Test
a. Knee flexed 90°, examiner pulls tibia anteriorly at the knee.
b. Positive sign = Increased anterior translation indicates ACL injury.
Tests for PCL Injury
1. Posterior Drawer Test
a. Knee flexed 90°, examiner pushes tibia posteriorly at the knee.
b. Positive sign = Posterior translation indicates PCL tear.
2. Godfrey (Posterior Sag) Test
a. Hip and knee flexed 90°, examiner observes tibial plateau at the knee.
b. Positive sign = Sagging tibia compared to contralateral knee indicates PCL injury.
Tests for Posterolateral Corner (PLC) Injury
1. Dial Test
a. Knee flexed 30° and 90°, examiner externally rotates tibia at the knee bilaterally.
b. Positive sign = ≥10° external rotation difference indicates PLC injury.
2. Reverse Pivot Shift Test
a. Examiner slowly extends knee while applying valgus + external rotation forces at the knee.
b. Positive sign = Clunk at ~30° flexion indicates PLC injury.

SPECIAL CONFIRMATORY TESTS – KNEE OSTEOARTHRITIS
Tests for Knee OA
1. Patellofemoral Compression Test (Grind Test)
a. Patient supine with knee extended. Examiner applies downward pressure on the patella while asking the patient to contract the quadriceps.
b. Positive sign = Pain or crepitus at the patellofemoral joint, suggesting patellofemoral OA.
2. Patellar Tilt Test
a. Examiner lifts the lateral edge of the patella while stabilizing the medial edge.
b. Positive sign = Limited tilt or pain indicates tight lateral structures or OA changes.
3. Crepitus Test
a. Examiner moves the knee through full flexion and extension, palpating and listening for audible or palpable crepitus.
b. Positive sign = Crepitus or grinding indicates articular cartilage degeneration.
4. Range of Motion Assessment
a. Examiner passively flexes and extends the knee while noting pain, stiffness, or limited ROM.
b. Positive sign = Reduced flexion or extension suggests joint degeneration.
5. Joint Line Tenderness
a. Examiner palpates medial and lateral tibiofemoral joint lines.
b. Positive sign = Pain at joint line indicates tibiofemoral OA involvement.
6. Functional Pain Assessment
a. Ask patient to perform activities such as squatting, stair climbing, or prolonged sitting.
b. Positive sign = Pain reproduction during these activities supports OA diagnosis.

SPECIAL CONFIRMATORY TESTS – PATELLA DISLOCATION
1. Patella Apprehension Test
a. Patient lies supine with the knee extended. Examiner gently pushes the patella laterally.
b. Positive sign = Patient shows apprehension or contracts quadriceps to prevent lateral displacement, indicating patellar instability.
2. Patella Tracking Assessment
a. Observe the patella during active knee flexion and extension.
b. Positive sign = Patella deviates laterally or fails to track smoothly within the trochlear groove, suggesting abnormal motion or previous dislocation.
3. Patellar Hyper-mobility Test
a. Patient lies supine, knee extended. Examiner gently displaces patella medially and laterally.
b. Positive sign = Patella moves excessively beyond normal range (>50% of patella width), indicating generalized patellar laxity.
4. Q-Angle Measurement
a. Measure the angle between the ASIS–patella line and patella–tibial tuberosity line with knee extended.
b. Positive sign = Q-angle increased (>15° in men, >20° in women), suggesting malalignment predisposing to lateral patellar dislocation.
NOTE:
Observation for Patella Alta / Genu Recurvatum
a. Inspect patella position in full extension and assess knee hyperextension.
b. Positive sign = High-riding patella (patella alta) or excessive hyperextension, indicating structural risk factors for dislocation.
Imaging (X-ray / MRI)
a. Lateral and axial X-rays; MRI if soft tissue assessment needed.
b. Positive sign = Subluxation/dislocation, osteochondral fractures, or medial patellofemoral ligament tear.

1. Straight Leg Raise Test
a. Patient lies supine with both legs extended. Examiner asks the patient to lift the affected leg straight up without bending the knee.

a. Patient lies supine with knee relaxed. Examiner palpates the patella gently using fingers to feel for tenderness, irregularity, or gap.

3. Active Knee Extension Test
a. Patient sits or lies supine. Examiner asks patient to actively straighten the knee from a flexed position.

4. Patellar Compression Test (Modified for fracture screening)
a. Patient lies supine with knee extended. Examiner gently presses patella posteriorly toward femur.
